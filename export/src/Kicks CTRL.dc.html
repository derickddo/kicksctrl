Why footwear resists recycling

A single shoe combines rubber, EVA foam, textiles, plastics, and adhesives — bonded together by design. Standard single-stream recycling can't separate them, so even collected shoes are downcycled or discarded. Footwear needs a dedicated separation process, which until now has not existed at scale.

Regulatory pressure
July 2026

The EU's ban on the destruction of unsold goods takes effect, extending EPR obligations to footwear producers. Brands will need audited recovery routes — not incineration — for end-of-life and unsold stock.

About

Building the missing layer of the footwear supply chain

Footwear is one of the most disposed-of consumer products on earth and one of the least recoverable. We exist to make it recoverable at industrial scale.

The thesis

Every recovery chain — paper, metal, glass, PET — was built by infrastructure, not by goodwill. Footwear has no equivalent. The shoe is a bonded composite of rubber, foam, textile, and plastics, which makes it nearly impossible to recycle through existing single-stream systems.

Kicks CTRL builds the dedicated layer that separates those materials and routes each into a clean, sellable stream — turning a waste liability into a supply of secondary raw material.

How we operate

We run a vertically integrated chain: aggregated collection, regional sorting facilities, and material processing with offtake agreements to industrial buyers. Volume is aggregated from brands meeting EPR obligations, retailer take-back, and municipal waste streams.

Operations are anchored in Africa, where formal recycling infrastructure is below 1% and where global footwear waste increasingly flows — positioning recovery capacity at the point of greatest accumulation.

Operating principles

Infrastructure first

We build physical capacity, not campaigns. Throughput is the product.

Material purity

Output is graded to spec so it competes with virgin feedstock, not just diverts it.

Auditable chain

Every tonne is tracked from intake to offtake for compliance-grade reporting.

Modular scale

Facilities replicate as standardized units, so capacity grows in predictable increments.
